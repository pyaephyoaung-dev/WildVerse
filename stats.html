A Timeline of Loss & Hope

Key milestones in wildlife conservation history.

1662
Dodo goes extinct
The first widely-documented species extinction caused by human activity — a warning the world largely ignored for centuries.
1914
Passenger Pigeon extinct
Martha, the last passenger pigeon, dies in Cincinnati Zoo. Once the most numerous bird in North America — now gone forever due to industrial hunting.
1948
IUCN Founded
The International Union for Conservation of Nature is established — the world's first global environmental organisation and keeper of the Red List.
1936
Thylacine extinct
The last known Tasmanian tiger dies in Hobart Zoo on 7 September, after the species was hunted to extinction across its entire range.
1963
IUCN Red List launched
The world's most comprehensive inventory of threatened species is published for the first time, giving conservation a scientific framework.
1970s
Bald Eagle near-extinction
Fewer than 500 nesting pairs remain due to DDT pesticide use and habitat loss. A landmark conservation success story begins.
1973
CITES adopted
The Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species comes into force, regulating the global wildlife trade and protecting thousands of species.
1995
Wolf reintroduction to Yellowstone
Gray wolves are reintroduced to Yellowstone National Park, triggering a "trophic cascade" that restored rivers, vegetation, and entire ecosystems.
2007
Bald Eagle delisted
The bald eagle is removed from the U.S. Endangered Species List after populations recover to over 10,000 nesting pairs — one of conservation's greatest victories.
2018
Last male Northern White Rhino dies
Sudan, the world's last male northern white rhino, dies in Kenya. Only two females remain. Scientists race to use assisted reproduction to save the subspecies.
2022
Kunming-Montréal Agreement
196 countries agree to protect 30% of Earth's land and oceans by 2030 — the most ambitious global biodiversity commitment in history.
2024
Mountain Gorilla population passes 1,000
Thanks to decades of sustained conservation and ecotourism, mountain gorilla numbers cross 1,000 individuals for the first time — a rare good news story.

Hawksbill Sea Turtle

Eretmochelys imbricata

The hawksbill sea turtle is critical to the health of coral reef ecosystems. Hunted nearly to extinction for their beautiful shells, they are now protected internationally.

Scientific NameEretmochelys imbricata
Class / OrderReptilia, Testudines
HabitatTropical Coral Reefs
DietOmnivore — sponges, algae, invertebrates
Weight46–70 kg
Size70–95 cm carapace length
SpeedUp to 35 km/h swimming
Lifespan30–50 years
RangeTropical Oceans
Conservation StatusLC — Least Concern
PopulationCritically endangered, tens of thousands

Interesting Facts

Their narrow beak allows them to extract sponges from crevices in coral reefs.
They are the primary predator of sea sponges, helping maintain coral reef balance.
Hawksbill shells were used in tortoiseshell products — a trade now internationally banned.
They can detect the Earth's magnetic field and use it to navigate across oceans.
